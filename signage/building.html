'Women'

Máydɨkmɨsé

'Men'

Tuulémɨsé

'Family, Relations'

Esak’ábe mi? (Do you know?) You may have noticed that each of the restroom signs ends in "mɨsé". In Nisenan, -mɨsé is a word-ending that refers to a group of people. Here, it goes on the end of kɨlé (woman) to form the word kɨlémɨsé (women), on máydɨk (man) to form the word máydɨkmɨsé (men), and on tuulé (relative, relation) to form the word tuulémɨsé (relatives, family).

The word-ending -mɨsé also goes on placenames to refer to the people of that location. You may have seen this ending before in words like Pusúunemɨsé and Yáalesmɨsé, which refer to the people of the villages of Pusúune and Yáales, respectively.

Esak’ábe mi? (Do you know?) You may have noticed that several of these signs end in "men". In Nisenan, -men is a word-ending that shows up on names for periods of time, including certain season names like Yoomén (Spring) and Okomén (Summer), month names like Lólmen, and other time expressions like mɨymén (that time, then).

Not all time expressions end in "men". For example, the word Tamás (Winter) does not contain this ending. It also is not to be confused with a different word-ending -men (no, not), which sounds the same but means something different entirely.

The SSBMI Community has ancestral connections to the Valley & Southern Hill dialects of the Nisenan language through the Tribe's Matriarchs, Pamela Cleanso Adams and Annie Hill Murray Paris. Pamela spoke Valley Nisenan and Annie likely spoke Southern Hill Nisenan. You can find out more about our efforts to revitalize the Nisenan and Northern Sierra Miwok languages by checking out our "Language FAQ" pamphlet.

If you have any questions about the language, please visit our offices in the Tribal Admin building or contact us at language@ssband.org.

For more help reading these words and phrases, please consult our Guide to the SSBMI Nisenan Alphabet.
