Family Songs

Hóde Meem?

Hóde Meem? (Where Are You Guys?) is a Nisenan-language song sung to the tune of "Brother John", also widely known as "Frère Jacques".

You can listen to the song in Nisenan using the controls below.

    Hóde Meem? (Where Are You Guys?)

    Nisenan
    Hedeedím ni, hedeedím ni,
    Hóde meem? Hóde meem?
    Banak’áy, c’enowá! Banak’áy, c’enowá!
    C’edaapé! C’edaapé!
    Hedeedím ni, hedeedím ni,
    Hóde meem? Hóde meem?
    Okóm bonnopɨtóm! Okóm bonnopɨtóm!
    K’aypepé! K’aypepé!
    English
    I am here, I am here,

    The sun is almost down! (x2)
    Let’s all have dinner! (x2)

About the lyrics

You might be wondering why we did not translate the actual lyrics of "Brother John/Frère Jacques" into Nisenan for this song. This is because the direct translation would be difficult to sing to the original tune, and would require words and phrases that don’t translate well directly. To make this song pleasant and easy to sing, we composed new lyrics that fit the original tune.

Language for everyday life

There are many parts of this song that you can expand on and use in everyday life!

For example, here are some useful phrases from this song (or using similar words) which you can use with your family:

Wake up!

(said to 1 person)

C’enóp!

Wake up, you guys!

(said to 2+ people)

C’enowá!

Let's all have breakfast!

C’edaapé!

Let's all have dinner!

K’aypepé!

How to say "Where are you guys?"

To ask where three or more people are ("Where are you guys?"), you can use the following question which is repeated throughout this song:

Where are you guys?

(said to 3+ people)

Hóde meem?

The sentence above is actually a short version of this question:

Where are you guys?

(said to 3+ people)

Homodíbe meem?

These two sentences mean exactly the same thing. The shorter version is just a conventional, casual way of asking this question in Nisenan.

Languages often have short versions of common expressions; for example, English speakers can say either "I will go" or "I'll go" depending on their preference in the moment. This pair of sentences, Homodíbe meem? and Hóde meem?, functions the same way.

To ask just one person where they are ("Where are you?"), you can say either of the following sentences:

Where are you?

(said to 1 person)

Hóde mi?

Where are you?

(said to 1 person)

Homodíbe mi?

To ask exactly two people where they are ("Where are you two?"), you can say either of the following sentences:

Where are you two?

(said to 2 people)

Hóde maam?

Where are you two?

(said to 2 people)

Homodíbe maam?

Words for "you"

As you can see from the sentences above, Nisenan has three different words for "you" depending on how many people you are addressing:

you

(1 person)

mi

you, you two

(2 people)

maam

you, you guys

(3 or more people)

meem

You may have already learned how to ask Homáabe mi? (How are you?) when greeting someone in Nisenan (check out our How are you feeling? page to learn more). Just like the question "Where are you?", you use different words for "you" depending on how many people you are asking:

How are you?

(said to 1 person)

Homáabe mi?

How are you two?

(said to 2 people)

Homáabe maam?

How are you guys?

(said to 3+ people)

Homáabe meem?
