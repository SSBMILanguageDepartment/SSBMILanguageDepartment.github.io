Positive Indian Parenting

Nisenan language for Tribal Member parents and caregivers

Unit 1: Traditional Parenting

May 11, 2026

This week, we learned three simple phrases that you can use with your children and other family members.

Try replacing the corresponding English phrases with these Nisenan phrases, and use them anytime you can. For example, you can tell your children wenném instead of telling them "thank you". You should encourage your children to use language too.

Tip: The more you use these phrases, the easier they will get until saying them becomes a normal part of your daily routines.

Séekas’in!

'Hello!'

Wenném.

'Thank you.'

Tuk’úm ni min.

'I love you.'

Tip: Try learning language with a partner, such as your spouse, another relative (e.g. grandparents, teenagers), or a friend. You can practice together, hold one another accountable to actually use the language, and have fun with a learning-partner.

Bonus

Here's another simple phrase you can use with your children; it sounds like you're saying "meh". Try using this phrase anytime you hand something to them, and over time it will become second nature for you to do so.

Me.

'Here you go. / Take it.'

Challenge

Try to use each phrase at least once per day this week. As you use them more, you'll find it gets easier to remember to do so.

Tip: Try making flashcards, or putting up signs in your house where you'll see them often to remind you to use language.

Additional information

There are many reasons to use language at home with your loved ones. For one, speaking your language is a way to connect with your Ancestors: The Nisenan language we share comes from the Tribe's Matriarchs and their relations, and by using language at home you are building and maintaining important connections with your Ancestors that you share with your children.

Maintaining these connections, such as through speaking your ancestral language, may promote positive mental health and have other benefits for your community too (e.g. source). Language learning is a lifetime journey, and you can't learn everything all at once, but even by using just a few phrases you help maintain your cultural identity and can be a positive role model for Youth.

Finally, language learning is fun. Your children may already know some of these phrases, especially if they go to the Ed Center or hang out with other kids who do, and they will be pleasantly surprised to hear you speaking language too. They will enjoy learning language with you and showing you how much they know, and they will see that speaking language makes you happy too.

Unit 2: Lessons of the Storyteller

May 18, 2026

This week, we learned a few names for relatives that you can use with your children and other family members.

Try replacing the corresponding English names with these Nisenan ones, and use them anytime you can. For example, try calling your children's grandma their kotó. It's easy to insert these names into English sentences too, and say things like "we're going to go see your kotó today" or "let's go to kotó's house". You should encourage your children to use these names too.

Tip: The more you use these names, the easier they will get until saying them feels totally natural to you and your family.

Grandparents

Kotó

'Grandma'

Paapá

'Grandpa (mother's father)'

Paa

'Grandpa (father's father)'

Heads-up: In Nisenan, you use different names for someone's maternal grandfather (i.e. their mother's father) compared to their paternal grandfather (i.e. their father's father). There are other Nisenan kinship terms like this (e.g. "aunt", "uncle"), and it may take some extra practice to get used to making these distinctions.

Parents

Ne

'Mom' (when talking about her)

Naa

'Mom' (when talking to her)

Heads-up: In Nisenan, you use different words for "mom" depending on whether you are talking about someone's mom (e.g. saying "My ne works at the clinic.") versus talking to your mom (e.g. saying "Hey, naa! How are you?").

Te

'Dad'

Children

Poo

'Daughter'

Tee

'Son'

Heads-up: Anytime you see the same letter written twice in a row, like in poo or tee, you pronounce that sound the same but hold it longer than usual. In Nisenan, sometimes there is a big difference in meaning depending on whether you hold a sound and say "te" (father) versus "tee" (son), or "po" (skin) versus "poo" (daughter).

Grandchildren

Pey

'Grandchild'

Tip: Try learning the language in chunks. Instead of learning all 9 words at once, first focus on the ones that are relevant to you.

Tip: Try learning language with a partner, such as your spouse, another relative (e.g. grandparents, teenagers), or a friend. You can practice together, hold one another accountable to actually use the language, and have fun with a learning-partner.

Bonus

You can combine these names with the phrases you learned last week. For example, you can combine tee (son) and tuk’úm ni min (I love you) to say tuk’úm ni min, tee (I love you, son). Try combining the names/phrases to expand the language you know!

Challenge

Use these names when talking to your children this week, and encourage them to use them with you too.

For example, you can use these names when talking to your children (e.g. "Séekas’in, tee! How was school today?"). You can also use them when talking about other relatives, such as when you are talking about your daily plans (e.g. "Tomorrow, we're going to your kotó's house") or telling stories (e.g. "When I was your age, your paapá would take me to Big Time").

Tip: Try drawing/labeling your family tree. This can be good practice, and you'll make a resource you can refer back to for help.

Additional resources

Check out our "Kind Words in Nisenan" flashcards. You can use these flashcards to learn names for other family members (e.g. "little sister", "husband"), as well as learn more positive phrases that you can use with your loved ones.

Stop by our offices in the Tribal Admin building to pick up a set of cards from our Language Nook. You can also email us at language@ssband.org to request a copy.

Unit 3: Lessons of the Cradleboard

June 1, 2026

This week, we learned the Nisenan words for "baby" and "cradlebasket", the Central California equivalent of a cradleboard.

P’at’á

'Baby'

Tɨtɨ́y

'Baby basket, Cradlebasket'

We also learned more names for relatives that you can use with your children and other family members, focusing on siblings.

Heads-up: In Nisenan, you use different names for your older siblings versus your younger siblings. These names are not related to one another, unlike in English where you use "sister" and "brother" for your siblings regardless of their age.

Older siblings

Etí

'Older sister'

Eyí

'Older brother'

Younger siblings

Əsə́w

'Younger sister' (Annie's dialect)

K’así

'Younger sister' (Pamela's dialect)

Heads-up: Some words differ between the Valley dialect, which Pamela Cleanso Adams spoke, and the Southern Hill dialect, which Annie Hill Murray Paris spoke. You may want to use the word for "younger sister" that your Matriarch used, but we encourage you to learn both words so that you understand when people use the other one too.

Tɨn

'Younger brother' (when talking about him)

Tɨ

'Younger brother' (when talking to him)

Heads-up: In Nisenan, you use different words for "younger brother" depending on whether you are talking about him (e.g. saying "My tɨn goes to in college.") versus talking to him (e.g. saying "Hey, tɨ! How are you?").

Try replacing the corresponding English names with these Nisenan ones. In our experience, Tribal Youth really enjoy using these words and figuring out whether they're older/younger than their cousins so they can use them as nicknames too.

Tip: Try learning the language in chunks. Instead of learning all 6 words at once, first focus on the ones that are relevant to you.

Tip: Try learning language with a partner, such as your spouse, another relative (e.g. grandparents, teenagers), or a friend. You can practice together, hold one another accountable to actually use the language, and have fun with a learning-partner.

Bonus

You can combine these names with the phrases you already know. For example, you can combine etí (big sister) and tuk’úm ni min (I love you) to say tuk’úm ni min, etí (I love you, big sis). Try combining the names/phrases to expand the language you know!

Challenge #1

Try drawing your family tree and labeling some of your tuulémɨsé (relatives, family) using the Nisenan names you know.

Here's an example family tree for a man named Ralph. Note that he doesn't have all the relations for which we've learned names.

Challenge #2

Use these names when talking to your children this week, and encourage them to use them with you too.

You can encourage your children to use these names by using them when you talk about their siblings. For example, you can use them when telling them to do things (e.g. "Go give this to your k’así") or when telling stories (e.g. "When your eyí was a baby, he used to cry and cry!"). The more you use these names, the more your children will use them too.

Additional resources

Check out our "Kind Words in Nisenan" flashcards. You can use these flashcards to learn names for other family members (e.g. "wife", "father"), as well as learn more positive phrases that you can use with your loved ones.

Unit 4: Harmony in Childrearing

June 15, 2026

This week, we learned how to ask how someone is doing/feeling in Nisenan:

Homáakani?

'How are you?'

(said to 1 person)

Heads-up: In Nisenan, you can also ask how someone is by saying Homáabe mi? Both of these phrases mean "How are you?"

We also learned a few responses you can use to share how you feel. There are many other ways that you could respond too:

Wenném ni.

'I am good.'

Wennemením ni.

'I am not good.'

Helúum ni.

'I am tired.'

Ok’óm ni.

'I am hungry.'

Wamayím ni.

'I am sick.'

Tip: Try learning the language in chunks. Instead of learning all 5 phrases at once, first learn wenném ni and another response.

Finally, we learned the phrase bomɨ́ɨ, which you can use to express sympathy with someone (like saying "oh, you poor thing"):

Bomɨ́ɨ.

'I'm sorry. / Oh, poor thing.'

(literal meaning: 'Pity/Sympathy')

Tip: Try learning language with a partner, such as your spouse, another relative (e.g. grandparents, teenagers), or a friend. You can practice together, hold one another accountable to actually use the language, and have fun with a learning-partner.

Challenge

Use these phrases when talking to your children this week, and encourage them to use them with you too.

For example, make a habit of asking your children Homáakani? (How are you?) anytime you see them. It's okay if they respond in English; that shows they're listening and understand what you asked. You can also use bomɨ́ɨ anytime you feel sorry for someone.

Additional resources

Check out our "feelings" poster and companion webpage to learn more ways to respond when someone asks how you feel.

Stop by our offices in the Tribal Admin building to pick up a copy. You can also email us at language@ssband.org.

The Nisenan language and the SSBMI Community

About the language: The SSBMI Community has ancestral ties to the Valley and Southern Hill dialects of the Nisenan language through the Tribe's Matriarchs, Pamela Cleanso Adams and Annie Hill Murray Paris. Pamela spoke Valley Nisenan and she, her brother Tom Cleanso, and her daughter Lillie Williams are responsible for passing on most of the knowledge we have of this dialect today. We have less direct info about Annie’s language; based on where she was from and language from her relations and associates, she likely spoke Southern Hill Nisenan.

The Nisenan language that we share is from speakers of the Valley dialect like Pamela and Tom as well as speakers of the Southern Hill dialect like William Joseph (a.k.a. Bill Joe), Ida Hill Starkey, and Charlie Hunchup. Valley Nisenan (Pamela's dialect) and Southern Hill Nisenan (Annie's dialect) are similar to one another in many respects, but when there are different but equivalent words from both dialects, we often share both.

We write Nisenan using a special alphabet. For more help reading the words and phrases we share, check out our alphabet guide.

You can learn more about the Nisenan language and its four dialects by checking out our "Language FAQ" pamphlet.
