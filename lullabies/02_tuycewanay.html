Lullabies

Túyc’e Wánay

Túyc’e Wánay is a Nisenan-language lullaby that was originally sung by Lizzie Enos, a Nisenan speaker from the Auburn area.

This lullaby consists of a single lyric, "túyc’e wánay", repeated.

You can hear Lizzie sing this lullaby using the controls below.

    Túyc’e Wánay, by Lizzie Enos

About the singer and song

This song was sung by Lizzie Enos, a Nisenan woman from the Auburn area, to linguist Richard Smith in the 1960s.

Lizzie was a speaker of the Central Hill dialect of Nisenan. In the 1960s, Smith and others worked with Lizzie to record her language, including to produce audio recordings of her oral stories and songs. The audio recording that we share here comes from the California Language Archive, at the University of California, Berkeley. The photo of Lizzie comes from the Phoebe A. Hearst Museum of Anthropology, also at UC Berkeley.

Lizzie's native dialect, Central Hill Nisenan, differs in many respects from the SSBMI's heritage Nisenan dialects: Valley Nisenan and Southern Hill Nisenan (which are more similar to one another). For example, Lizzie's word túyc’e would be tuyíc’e in these dialects. While these words are still similar, in other respects the words and grammar of Central Hill Nisenan are completely different from Valley/Southern Hill Nisenan.

Lizzie was a close friend of Lillie Williams, the daughter of SSBMI Matriarch Pamela Adams. We are excited to get to share her language too.

Language for everyday life

You can expand on and use parts of this song in everyday life!

For example, tuy- (which is part of the word túyc’e) is a Nisenan word root related to 'sleep'. Here are some Nisenan phrases with this root:

English Nisenan

Sleep!

(You're telling someone to go to sleep)

Tuy!

Sleep well.

(You're telling someone to sleep well)

Wentín tuy.
