Homáabe mi?      Kucí’’acin aa?      How are you?

On this webpage, you can learn how to ask how someone is feeling and how to respond in Nisenan and Miwok.

You can ask how someone is feeling using one of the following phrases. Note that, when speaking Nisenan or Miwok, you use different language depending on whether you are asking 1 person or a group of people how they feel:

English Nisenan Miwok

How are you?

(said to 1 person)

Homáabe mi?

Kucí’’acin aa?

You can respond in any of a number of ways. For example, here are some phrases that you might use to share how you feel in Nisenan or Miwok:

I am good.

Kucít.

I am not good.

Kucí’wat.

I am happy.

I am unhappy/sad.

Eyáhŋem.

I am angry.

(said by a woman)

Osá’ŋem.

I am angry.

(said by a man)

Əəním ni.

I am scared/nervous.

Bɨɨk’ɨ́m ni.

I am healthy/well.

Esak’ábe mi? (Do you know?)

You may have noticed that some pairs of phrases that have opposite meanings in English differ only slighty in Nisenan/Miwok. For example, compare Nisenan "Kɨɨpém ni" (I am happy) with "Kɨɨpemením ni" (I am not happy), or Miwok "Kucít" (I am good) with "Kucí’wat" (I am not good). In Nisenan, the suffix "-men" means "no, not". Conversely, in Miwok the suffix "-wa" is one of several that means "no, not".

You may have also noticed that women and men say different things when they are angry. In Nisenan and Miwok, you use different words to talk about a "woman" being angry versus a "man" being angry. Namely, a woman (but not a man) would share their anger by saying "Hont’owím ni" in Nisenan or "Osá’ŋem" in Miwok. Conversely, a man (but not a woman) would say "Əəním ni" in Nisenan or "Naŋá’ŋem" in Miwok. In Miwok, these words are actually related to the words "óssa" (woman) and "náŋŋa" (man). Of course, a man would still use the "woman"-words to talk about a woman who is angry, and vice versa a woman would use the "man"-angry words to talk about a man who is angry.

The Miwok phrase "Yɨtɨ́ttɨttɨm" (I am scared/nervous) is a fun phrase to say. In Miwok, the word "yɨtɨ́ttɨttɨ-" imitates the sound of someone's teeth chattering. So, when you say "Yɨtɨ́ttɨttɨm", you are literally saying "I am teeth-chattering"!
