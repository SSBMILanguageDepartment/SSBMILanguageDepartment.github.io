Beading Words in Nisenan and Northern Sierra Miwok

The SSBMI Exhibits & Collections Center (ECC) hosted its first Read & Bead where participants were able to create beaded jewelry on February 13, 2025. Here, you can learn Nisenan and Northern Sierra Miwok beading terms that the SSBMI Language Department shared at the event.

Nisenan

The SSBMI community has ancestral ties to the Valley and Southern Hill dialects of the Nisenan language through the Tribe's Matriarchs, Pamela Cleanso Adams and Annie Hill Murray Paris. Pamela and her relations spoke Valley Nisenan and shared some of their knowledge of the Nisenan language through historical documents. We have less direct information about Annie's language; however, based on where she was from and the documented language of her relations and associates, it is likely that Annie spoke Southern Hill Nisenan.

The Nisenan language that we share is from speakers of the Valley dialect like Pamela Cleanso Adams and her brother Tom Cleanso as well as speakers of the Southern Hill dialect like William Joseph, Ida Hill Starkey, & Charlie Hunchup. The words below are from Southern Hill Nisenan. We have less information on this topic from speakers of Valley Nisenan; if you want to know more, please contact us at language@ssband.org.

Awaahá

'Needle' (i.e. metal needle)

K’uk’

'Cordage' (includes rope, string, thread)

Howwotú

'Bead' (includes small beads, glass beads)

Esak’ábe mi? (Do you know?) Nisenan speakers borrowed the word awaahá (needle) from the Spanish word for needle, aguja. Bone needles and awls are called p’amc’í in both Valley and Southern Hill Nisenan.

Northern Sierra Miwok

The Northern Sierra Miwok language that we share comes from many speakers like Eph Cummings and others who worked with C. Hart Merriam in the early 1900s, and Queenie Miller, Elena McCauley, & Nicolas Villa Sr. who worked with linguist Catherine Callaghan on the Northern Sierra Miwok dictionary (1987). If you want to know more about the words we share below, please contact us at language@ssband.org.

Awóoha

'Needle' (i.e. metal needle)

Híilo

'Thread, String'

Hóowoṭu

'Bead' (includes small beads, glass beads)

Hɨ́y’ɨksɨs aa? (Do you know?) Northern Sierra Miwok speakers borrowed the word awóoha (needle) from the Spanish word for needle, aguja. They also borrowed the word híilo (thread, string) from the Spanish word for thread, hilo.

Esak’ahá daak’ábe mi c’aykɨ́ɨ? (Do you want to know more?)

For more help reading these words, please consult our Guide to the SSBMI Nisenan Alphabet or our Guide to the SSBMI Miwok Alphabet.

If you have any questions about the language, please contact us at language@ssband.org.
