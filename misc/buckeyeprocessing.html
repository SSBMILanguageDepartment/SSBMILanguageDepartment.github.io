Buckeye Ball Processing

Buckeye balls are a traditional food source for many Native people in Central California. Both buckeye balls and the California Buckeye tree which they grow on are called poló in Nisenan and úunu in Northern Sierra Miwok.

Poló

'Buckeye ball, California Buckeye' (Nisenan)

Úunu

'Buckeye ball, California Buckeye' (Northern Sierra Miwok)

California Buckeye balls (photo by John Morgan)

Buckeye balls contain poisonous neurotoxic glycosides which must be removed prior to eating. The Nisenan and other Native peoples of Central California have several methods to make buckeye balls safe for consumption which involve lengthy cooking and/or leaching in water.

For example, to make buckeye mush the nuts are boiled in a basket using hot rocks. The cooked mush is leached in a sand- or leaf-lined basin or leached overnight in a stream. This process removes the toxins, making the mush safe to eat.

Lizzie Enos circa 1961 (photo by Samuel A. Barrett)

Lizzie Enos was a Nisenan woman from the Auburn area who spoke the Central Hill dialect of Nisenan. She was a close friend of Lillie Williams, who was one of the daughters of SSBMI Matriarch Pamela Cleanso Adams. Lizzie Enos and Lillie Williams used to go gather and speak the Nisenan language together.

In the 1920s-1960s, Lizzie Enos worked with several different anthropologists and linguists to share her knowledge of Nisenan language, culture, history, and lifeways. Her native dialect differs in many respects from the heritage dialects of the SSBMI Community, i.e. Valley Nisenan & Southern Hill Nisenan; however, we learn a lot from the knowledge she shared about traditional cultural practices. In 1965, American Indian Films poduced a documentary of Lizzie Enos showing how to process buckeye balls, which you can watch at the link above.

American Indians Films was the brainchild of Samuel A. Barrett, a UC Berkeley anthropologist and linguist who later worked as the Director of the Milwaukee Public Museum. In the 1960s, towards the end of his career, Barrett and other anthropologists were concerned that there were becoming fewer and fewer opportunities for anthropology students to learn about traditional practices directly from Native people. To address this, Barrett worked with cultural practitioners to record these practices and publish documentary films via the American Indian Films project.

From 1961 to 1964, Barrett and American Indian Films published 15 documentary films. This includes the film of Lizzie Enos processing buckeye balls, films about the processing of other foods like acorns and pine nuts, a series of films on Pomo basketmaking, and much more. In addition to the published films, Barrett recorded over 360,000 feet of unpublished 16mm footage through American Indian Films. You can read more about Samuel A. Barrett and the American Indian Films project, including find a complete list of the published documentaries, here.

While our interests are quite different from those of Barrett, who was concerned about the growing lack of firsthand learning opportunities for college anthropology students, we are able to learn a lot about these practices in order to revitalize them thanks to the films he helped create.
