TEK Map Logo

The SSBMI Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) Department's logo features a map of the Sacramento Valley, focusing on some of the waterways and ancestral Nisenan villages that are special to the SSBMI community and are the focus of TEK's water advocacy work.

The TEK Department collaborated with the Language Department to include Nisenan names for the waterways & villages on the TEK Map. Here, you can listen to the names on this map and learn more about these places and TEK's and the Tribe's cultural reclamation efforts.

You can also learn more about the language on the map here.

About the TEK Department: Traditional Ecological Knowledge or TEK refers to the evolving knowledge acquired by Indigenous people over hundreds or even thousands of years through direct contact with the environment. Learning about the ways our ancestors appropriated plants and animals for cultural uses while allowing them to flourish helps us change the ways in which we interact with nature today.

The SSBMI TEK Department is part of the Tribe's Cultural Resources Division with the Language Department. They work closely to weave the Tribe's ancestral Nisenan language back into cultural practices.

What do you call a river? English speakers today can find a river's official English name on maps and signs. Traditionally, Nisenan people named rivers descriptively from their own perspectives. The names that we share here come from Tom Cleanso, who lived at Pusúune at the confluence of the Sacramento and American Rivers, and they describe those rivers from his point of view: Táamom ("West Water"; Sacramento River) and Nótowmom ("East Water"; American River). Other speakers, who live in other places, have other names that describe the rivers differently.

Similarly, Tom's name for the Cosumnes River is grounded in his dialect: Tom and his sister, SSBMI Matriarch Pamela Cleanso Adams, spoke the Valley dialect of the Nisenan language. Valley Nisenan speakers call rivers mómti, which you'll see in his name Koosóm Mómti (Cosumnes River). Speakers of other Nisenan dialects call rivers sew, and this is reflected in some of the river names that other Nisenan speakers have shared. For example, Southern Hill Nisenan speaker William Joseph (a.k.a. Bill Joe) calls the Cosumnes River Koosóm Sew.

All this is to say that there is not just one "correct" Nisenan name for any given river. We are proud to share Tom Cleanso's names because he is a close relative of the Tribe, but we also respect that other speakers, including Tom's relations, have shared other names for these rivers too. Reclaiming the Nisenan language entails recognizing that traditional naming practices allow us to name the waterways in a multitude of ways.

Esak’ábe mi? (Do you know?) Wóllok was an important space for the Tribe's ancestors, including our Nisenan grandmothers and Hawaiian grandfathers, for whom it served as a safe haven first as a Nisenan village site and later as a Hawaiian fishing village.

In 2020, the Tribe reclaimed Wóllok by purchasing the Verona Marina. In April 2020, the Tribe held its Spring Dance and brought ceremony back to Wóllok. Being here at Wóllok gives us an opportunity to teach Tribal Members about our traditional ways & how our ancestors utilized the waterways and the ecosystem to continue their traditional cultural practices.

About the language:As we noted above, the river names that we share here come from Tom Cleanso. Tom was a Nisenan man from Pusúune who lived his life near there, at the confluence of the Sacramento and American Rivers, and his river names refer to them from his own perspective.

More generally, the Nisenan language that we share comes from speakers of the Valley dialect of Nisenan like SSBMI Matriarch Pamela Cleanso Adams and her brother Tom Cleanso, as well as speakers of the Southern Hill dialect like William Joseph, Ida Hill Starkey, and Charlie Hunchup.

Valley Nisenan and Southern Hill Nisenan are very similar to one another in terms of their words & grammar. To the best of our knowledge, the words and phrases that we share on this website are shared by or would be understood by the speakers of both dialects except where noted.

The Tribe has ancestral ties to the Nisenan language through the Tribe's Matriarchs, Pamela Cleanso Adams and Annie Hill Murray Paris. Pamela, Annie, and many of their close relations spoke Nisenan and shared their language with researchers in the early 1900s. The knowledge they shared provides us with a pathway to reconnect with the Nisenan language today. The Tribe also has significant connections to ancestral Nisenan villages like Pusúune, K’ademmá, Wóllok, and Yáales, where the Nisenan language has been spoken since time immemorial.

We hope that you will join our departments in reconnecting with this part of the Tribe's heritage. Nisenáanweypé! (Let's speak Nisenan!)
