Language Notes

The SSBMI Community has ancestral ties to the Valley and Southern Hill dialects of the Nisenan language through the Tribe's Matriarchs, Pamela Cleanso Adams and Annie Hill Murray Paris.

Pamela spoke the Valley dialect and she, her brother Tom Cleanso, and her daughter Lillie Williams are responsible for passing on most of the knowledge we have of Valley Nisenan today. We have less direct information about Annie’s language; based on where she was from and documented language from her relations and associates, she likely spoke Southern Hill Nisenan.

Today, we work to revitalize these dialects while navigating dialect variation in an intentional manner. When words are strongly linked to place, we use the word that is most closely tied to our location. For example, speakers of Valley Nisenan and Southern Hill Nisenan have shared different words for "river", and we respect this variation by using different words for "river" for projects based in the Sacramento Valley (mómti) versus in the foothills, including the location of the Shingle Springs Rancheria (sew).

What do you call the rivers?

English speakers today can find a river's official English name on maps and signs. Traditionally, Nisenan people name rivers descriptively from their own perspectives. The names we most often share come from Tom Cleanso, who lived at Pusúune at the confluence of the Sacramento and American Rivers, and his names for those rivers describe them from his point of view: Táamom ("West Water") and Nótowmom ("East Water").

However, other speakers, who live in other places, use other names that describe the rivers differently. For example, William Joseph (a.k.a. Bill Joe), a Nisenan man from Amador County, called the Sacramento River the Wollokóm Sew and the American River the K’ademmám Sew, naming them after major villages on their courses, Wóllok and K’ademmá. Other speakers who spoke the same dialects shared different names too.

Nisenan

Wollokóm Sew

English

Sacramento River

(literal: Wóllok River)

K’ademmám Sew

American River

(literal: K’ademmá River)

All this is to say that there is not just one "correct" Nisenan name for any given river. We are proud to share Tom Cleanso's names because he is a close relative of the Tribe, but we also respect that other speakers, including Tom's relations, have shared other names for these rivers too. Reclaiming the Nisenan language entails recognizing that traditional naming practices allow us to name the waterways in a multitude of ways.
